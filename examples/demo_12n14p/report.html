the machine

Architecture

A 12-slot / 14-pole fractional-slot concentrated winding: each tooth is one continuous spiral coil, phased and signed by the star-of-slots layout, giving a fundamental winding factor kw1 = 0.939. Two stator boards sandwich a single rotor disk and are wired in series, so their torques add at the same current. There is no iron: no cogging, no saturation, and honestly not much inductance either -- that bill arrives in the drive section.

The design hunt

Trace width is the knob that matters most, so the sweep below re-runs the full engine at each base width with everything else held fixed: narrow traces buy turns (Kt and inductance), wide traces buy current (cooler copper), and nothing buys enough inductance to matter against the ripple gate. The committed board doesn't pick one width — its copper starts at 0.15 mm at the inner radius and tapers to 0.75 mm at the rim, matching the production tapered-wedge artwork in the viewer above.

Each point is a full engine re-evaluation of the design at that trace width. The marked width is the one this design committed to.

Physics, from the real geometry

Everything here is computed from the actual geometry: vectorised Biot-Savart on the Amperian current-loop magnets gives the airgap field; the Lorentz force on every discretised copper segment, under proper 3-phase commutation, gives torque. The field map is the torque-coupling component B_z at the front copper plane.

the honest part

Can you actually drive it?

The part most PCB-motor write-ups skip: an air-core winding has tiny inductance, so a stock FOC drive's PWM turns into large ripple current. The gate compares worst-case ripple v_bus / (4·L·f_pwm) against a budget of 30% of the continuous current.

the fine print

Verdict, honestly

As a motor, it's an honest middleweight: ~17 mNm continuous (±30%) from two ordinary 2-layer boards. As a demo, it's complete: this page is what every pcb-motor showcase gives you for free — spin the rotor, zoom the copper, pull the sandwich apart, and read the part where the tool tells you you still need three chokes.

Model honesty: this is an analytical, feasibility-grade model — treat absolute torque as ±30%. The field solver is validated to <1% against closed-form solutions; the error budget is dominated by magnet Br tolerance and fringing, your actual assembled air gap (the single most sensitive parameter), and copper etching variation. Relative comparisons between designs are much better than ±30%. Calibrate against FEMM or a bench coil before committing money to a build.
